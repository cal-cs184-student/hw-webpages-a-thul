Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Effects of Changing Parameters

I experimented with changing the spring constant (ks), density, and damping parameters to observe their effects on the cloth simulation. Below are descriptions and screenshots showing the differences.

Spring Constant (ks)

With a low ks, the cloth is very flexible and stretchy. It droops a lot under gravity and has more pronounced folds and wrinkles. With a high ks, the cloth is much stiffer and holds its shape more rigidly, with less sagging and fewer wrinkles. The springs resist deformation more strongly at higher values.

Density

Increasing density makes each point mass heavier, so gravity pulls the cloth down more. The cloth sags more and the top edge droops a ton. With low density, the cloth is more "floaty", maintaining its shape with less deformation. The effect of density is somewhat inverse to that of ks: higher density behaves similarly to lower spring constant in terms of sagging.

Damping

With low damping, the cloth oscillates for a long time before settling, swinging back and forth dramatically. while high damping makes it glide to its rest without oscillation like there's a mild breeze.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

Collision Implementation

For sphere collisions, I check whether a point mass has moved inside the sphere by comparing its distance from the sphere's origin to the radius. If it has intersected, I compute where it should be on the sphere's surface (extending the vector from the origin through the point mass to the surface), and then apply a correction vector scaled down by friction to nudge the point mass back to just outside the sphere's surface.

For plane collisions, I check whether a point mass has crossed the plane between the current and last time step by checking the sign of the dot product of both positions with the plane's normal. If it has crossed, I compute the tangent point where the path intersects the plane, and apply a correction to place the point mass slightly above the plane surface (offset by SURFACE_OFFSET in the direction of the normal), again scaled by friction.

Cloth on Sphere at Different ks Values

At ks = 500, the cloth almost swathes itself over the sphere, conforming closely to its shape with many folds and wrinkles hanging down. At the default ks = 5000, the cloth drapes over more naturally. At ks = 50000, the cloth is much stiffer, and doesn't conform nearly as well.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

Self-Collision Implementation

I implemented self-collision handling using spatial hashing. The 3D space is divided into boxes of size w * h * t (based on the number of point masses and the bounding box dimensions), and each point mass is hashed into a box (implemented by x + y * 10000 + z * 100000000 so digits are directly encoded in the hash) When checking for self-collisions, for each point mass, I look up candidate point masses in the same hash bucket and check if any are within 2 * t distance. If so, I computed a correction vector to push them apart to at least 2 * t distance, and average all such corrections (excluding the mass itself) to apply the final adjustment, scaled down by simulation_steps.

Cloth Falling and Folding on Itself

Varying Parameters

With higher density, the cloth falls faster and crumples on itself, creating more layers of folds and wrinkles. With lower density, the cloth falls more gently and the folds are wider, rounder and more spread out. With a higher ks, the cloth resists folding and the folds are rounder and more spread out again. the cloth tries to maintain its flat shape. With a lower ks, the cloth crumples more easily, similar to the effect of increasing the density.

Part 5: Shaders

Shader Programs

A shader program runs on the GPU and computes the visual appearance of each fragment (pixel) on screen. It consists of two main stages: the vertex shader and the fragment shader. The vertex shader runs first and processes each vertex of the geometry, by transforming vertex positions from model space to screen space. It sends interpolated data like normals, texture coordinates, and positions to the fragment shader. The fragment shader then runs for every pixel covered by a triangle and determines the final color of that pixel, using the interpolated values from the vertex shader along with lighting calculations, texture lookups, etc.

Blinn-Phong Shading Model

The Blinn-Phong model computes lighting as the sum of three components: ambient, diffuse, and specular. The ambient component (\( k_a I_a \)) provides a constant base illumination regardless of light direction. The diffuse component (\( k_d (I/r^2) \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{l}) \)) simulates light scattering equally in all directions from a surface, depending on the angle between the surface normal and the light direction. The specular component (\( k_s (I/r^2) \max(0, \mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{h})^p \)) creates shiny highlights using the half-vector between the light and view directions, with the exponent \( p \) controlling the tightness of the highlight.

Bump Mapping vs. Displacement Mapping

Bump mapping modifies the surface normals based on a height map texture without actually changing the geometry, creating the illusion of surface detail. On the other hand, displacement mapping moves the vertices of the mesh according to the height map, physically altering the geometry to the viewer (which is why we can see divots and bumps in the sphere).

Coarseness Comparison (-o 16 -a 16 vs. -o 128 -a 128)

With the coarser mesh (-o 16 -a 16), bump mapping looks essentially the same since it only modifies normals, but displacement mapping looks blocky and jagged because there aren't enough vertices to capture the fine detail of the height map. With the finer mesh (-o 128 -a 128), displacement mapping produces much smoother, more detailed surface adjustments (see the grooves of the brick pattern) that closely match the height map texture, while bump mapping again looks similar regardless of mesh resolution.
